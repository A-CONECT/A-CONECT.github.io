Children engaging in imaginary play.
Facts:
When children take on a pretend role, they further develop their ability to see other perspectives and be empathetic. 
Playing make believe is an excellent way to expose children to new vocabulary, such as "castle" and "rescue."
Research has suggested that simple toys, such as blocks or toy cars, promote more imaginative play. 
Possible Questions:
(SM) 
Did you or someone you know have imaginary friends? 
Did you play make-believe games? What games? 
What are some common characters in make-believe games?
(EP) 
Have you ever played a make-believe game with a child? What game was it? 
Did you or someone you know ever participate in a school play or skit as a child? What was their role? 
Did you ever visit or take children to visit fictional characters like Santa Claus, the Easter Bunny, or Disney Princesses? 
Do you remember when you found out that Santa Claus wasn't real? How did you react?
(EX) 
Do you think children should be encouraged to play and use their imagination? In what settings, or at what age, might this not be appropriate? 
Should parents be expected to tell their kids about hard reality (Santa Claus, Tooth Fairy, etc.)? At what age do you think it is okay to tell kids the truth about these characters? 
Many people stop playing make-believe as they grow up. Do you think that adults should engage in make-believe activities such as improvisation? Why/why not?
